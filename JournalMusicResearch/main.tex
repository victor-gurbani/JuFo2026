\begin{abstract}
This study presents a computational framework for quantifying stylistic evolution in Western art music, analysing 144 solo piano compositions by Johann Sebastian Bach, Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart, Fr\'ed\'eric Chopin, and Claude Debussy sourced from the PDMX dataset. A pipeline built on the music21 toolkit extracts 36 features spanning harmonic, melodic, and rhythmic dimensions. One-way ANOVA with Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate (BH-FDR) correction reveals that 29 of 36 features differ significantly across composers ($q < 0.05$). Principal component analysis reduces the feature space to three components explaining 48.2\% of variance, visualising a stylistic continuum rather than discrete clusters. A difference-in-differences model captures the dramatic acceleration of stylistic change during the Romantic era (termed the \emph{Romantic Reversal}), while a novel triple-difference analysis, conditioning on mode, demonstrates that minor-key compositions drove this acceleration more strongly than major-key works. External validation, projecting works by Joe Hisaishi and Maurice Ravel into the embedding space, confirms the framework's generalisability beyond the training corpus.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Can musical style be quantified computationally? Western art music evolved through
distinct stylistic periods, each characterised by identifiable harmonic, melodic, and
rhythmic conventions. The contrapuntal rigour of the Baroque, the homophonic clarity of
the Classical era, the expressive chromaticism of the Romantic period, and the timbral and harmonic innovations of Impressionism form a rich trajectory of aesthetic transformation.
This study examines four composers whose works epitomise these successive eras:
Johann Sebastian Bach (Baroque), Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (Classical),
Fr\'ed\'eric Chopin (Romantic), and Claude Debussy (Impressionist). By extracting
interpretable features from symbolic scores and subjecting them to rigorous statistical
analysis, the present work seeks to transform qualitative musicological intuition into
empirical measurement.

Computational musicology has matured considerably since early rule-based attempts at
stylistic analysis. Modern toolkits such as music21 \citep{Cuthbert2010} provide
programmable access to symbolic score representations, while large-scale datasets
including the Public Domain MusicXML archive \citep[PDMX;][]{PDMX2024}, containing over
254,000 scores, supply the raw material for corpus-level inquiry. Recent surveys confirm
sustained interest in style-based composer identification \citep{Simonetta2025}, though
most published approaches prioritise discriminative performance over musicological
interpretability. Among early statistical studies, the work of
\citet{LinJeng1987} stands closest to the present investigation: that 1987 thesis
attempted to distinguish Mozart, Chopin, Debussy, and Webern through Prolog-based pattern
rules applied to manually encoded DARMS data, yet its small sample size and limited
tooling constrained both scope and generalisability.

Despite this growing body of work, a significant gap persists. The majority of
computational studies frame the problem as one of composer classification \citep{Herremans2015}, treating
predictive accuracy as the primary measure of success. Such an orientation, while
valuable for benchmarking, leaves the underlying dynamics of stylistic change largely
unexamined. In particular, three analytical dimensions remain underexplored when
considered together: (a)~comprehensive, interpretable feature extraction spanning
harmonic, melodic, and rhythmic domains; (b)~static significance testing through omnibus
ANOVA with BH-FDR correction and dimensionality reduction via principal
component analysis (PCA); and (c)~dynamic evolutionary modelling that captures not
merely \emph{whether} styles differ, but \emph{how} and \emph{when} the rate of
stylistic change accelerates or reverses. The temporal dimension of musical
evolution, specifically the velocity and direction of parametric shifts across
centuries, has received comparatively less quantitative attention than static classification tasks.

This study makes four principal contributions to the field. First, it introduces a
36-feature extraction pipeline spanning harmonic, melodic, and rhythmic dimensions,
applied to a balanced corpus of 144 solo piano works (36 per composer) drawn from the
PDMX archive. Second, it maps static stylistic differences through one-way ANOVA with
Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate (FDR) correction \citep{Benjamini1995} and
Tukey HSD post-hoc comparisons, complemented by a three-component PCA projection that
visualises the stylistic continuum. Third, it proposes a dynamic evolutionary model
rooted in difference-in-differences (DD) analysis, quantifying the magnitude and
direction of stylistic acceleration at each epochal boundary. A novel extension, the
triple-difference (DDD) analysis, conditions on mode (major versus minor) to isolate
tonality-specific evolutionary trends. Fourth, it validates the framework through
external case studies, projecting works by Joe Hisaishi and Maurice Ravel into the
established embedding space to assess generalisability beyond the training corpus.

The remainder of this article is organised as follows. \Cref{sec:litreview} surveys
related work in computational musicology and positions the present study within that
landscape. \Cref{sec:methods} details the corpus curation process, feature extraction
pipeline, and statistical methodology. \Cref{sec:results} presents findings from both
the static and dynamic analyses. \Cref{sec:discussion} interprets these results in
musicological context and evaluates validation case studies. \Cref{sec:conclusion}
summarises contributions and outlines directions for future research.

\section{Literature Review}\label{sec:litreview}

The computational analysis of musical style rests on software tools capable of
parsing symbolic representations at scale. \citet{McKay2006} introduced jSymbolic,
a feature extractor for MIDI files that computes over 100 descriptors for genre
and style classification tasks. The music21 toolkit \citep{Cuthbert2010}, written
in Python, broadened the analytical scope by providing direct access to MusicXML
score objects, enabling harmonic analysis, Roman numeral labelling, and voice-leading
computations within a single framework. For MATLAB-based workflows,
\citet{EerolaToiviainen2004} developed the MIDI Toolbox, which supports pitch,
rhythmic, and tonal analyses. Together, these tools transformed computational
musicology from a niche pursuit into a reproducible, corpus-level discipline, though
differences in input format and feature semantics complicate cross-study comparisons.

Statistical corpus studies have established quantitative baselines for understanding
tonal practice across historical periods. \citet{White2013} analysed statistical
properties of tonality from 1650 to 1900, documenting systematic shifts in harmonic
vocabulary. Information-theoretic frameworks offer a complementary lens:
\citet{PearceWiggins2012} modelled auditory expectation through entropy and
surprisal, demonstrating that listeners internalise probabilistic regularities
of the music they encounter. \citet{Temperley2007} formalised the notion of style
as probability distribution, treating compositional choices as draws from
genre-specific statistical models. From the perspective of music history,
\citet{Meyer1989} provided a theoretical account of style that integrates
ideological and cognitive dimensions, arguing that stylistic norms simultaneously
constrain and enable creative expression. These studies collectively demonstrate
that musical style is amenable to quantitative description, though they tend to
focus on broad historical trends rather than pairwise composer comparisons.

Several music-theoretic contributions directly inform the feature design of the
present study. \citet{VonHippel2000} refined the concept of pitch proximity by
distinguishing tessitura from mobility, a distinction reflected here in the
separation of pitch range from interval-based melodic descriptors.
\citet{LerdahlJackendoff1983} proposed a generative theory of tonal music that
formalises hierarchical structures of rhythm and pitch, providing the theoretical
basis for features such as downbeat emphasis and metrical regularity.
\citet{Huron2006} extended expectation-based models to encompass a wide range
of perceptual phenomena, reinforcing the idea that quantifiable surface features
encode cognitively meaningful stylistic information.

Despite substantial progress on individual fronts, no prior study combines static
significance testing (omnibus ANOVA with FDR correction and PCA-based dimensionality
reduction) with dynamic evolutionary modelling (difference-in-differences and
triple-difference analysis) on a balanced symbolic corpus. Most computational
approaches treat classification as the analytical endpoint; the present study
instead treats statistical significance and evolutionary dynamics as primary tools
of inquiry. The DD/DDD framework, widely used in econometrics to isolate treatment
effects from confounding trends, has not previously been applied to musicological
data. By adapting this methodology, the present work quantifies not only
\emph{whether} composers differ, but \emph{at what rate} and \emph{in which
direction} stylistic parameters shifted across epochal boundaries.

\section{Materials and Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Corpus Curation}

The analytical corpus was drawn from the Public Domain MusicXML archive
\citep[PDMX;][]{PDMX2024}, a large-scale collection of 254,077 symbolic scores
hosted on Zenodo. Because the archive aggregates heterogeneous contributions
(orchestral reductions, ensemble arrangements, multi-movement compilations, and
duplicate transcriptions), substantial curation was required before meaningful
statistical analysis could proceed.

A multi-stage filtering pipeline, implemented in Python, narrowed the archive to
solo piano works suitable for balanced cross-composer comparison. First,
composer names were normalised through token-based alias matching, resolving
variant spellings and excluding entries attributed to family members sharing a
surname (e.g.\ C.P.E.\ Bach). Second, licence and quality filters retained only
scores flagged as deduplicated, free of licensing conflicts, and representing the
best unique arrangement of each work. Third, instrumentation was verified through
a combination of structured JSON metadata fields and music21 instrument detection,
ensuring that only genuine solo piano scores entered the corpus. Orchestral
reductions, chamber arrangements, and vocal accompaniments were discarded at
this stage.

Four composers were selected to represent successive stylistic periods in Western
art music: Johann Sebastian Bach (Baroque), Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (Classical),
Fr\'ed\'eric Chopin (Romantic), and Claude Debussy (Impressionist). To prevent
class imbalance from distorting downstream statistics, the corpus was clipped to
the smallest number of qualifying works per composer, yielding 36 works each and
144 scores in total, a sample size consistent with established evaluation norms in empirical music research \citep{Urbano2016}. The resulting corpus spans 21,344 measures and 71,585
quarter-note durations (approximately 13.3 hours at a reference tempo of
90~BPM). Preliminary checks with relaxed filter thresholds suggested
that loosening the quality or licence constraints increased corpus size at the
cost of introducing scoring artefacts and composer-level bias, validating the
conservative filtering strategy adopted here.

Beethoven's oeuvre spans and actively drives the transition from the Classical to
the Romantic stylistic paradigm. Including his works would confound the distinct
pre- and post-shift states required by the differencing framework adopted in this
study. The four selected composers instead represent prototypical exemplars of
their respective periods, maximising construct validity for the between-era
comparisons that follow.

\subsection{Feature Extraction}

Each MusicXML score was loaded and parsed using the music21 toolkit
\citep{Cuthbert2010}. The extraction pipeline computed 36 numerical features per
score, organised into three analytical domains: harmonic (16 features), melodic
(11 features), and rhythmic (9 features). All features are deterministic and
fully reproducible given the same input scores. Scores that raised parsing
exceptions were logged and skipped rather than halting the pipeline, ensuring
robustness across the heterogeneous PDMX archive. The complete feature inventory
is summarised in \Cref{tab:features}.

\subsubsection{Harmonic Features}

Harmonic analysis begins by collapsing each polyphonic score into a single chord
stream via music21's \texttt{chordify()} method, which identifies simultaneous
sonorities at every rhythmic onset. From this stream, 16 features capture the
vertical harmonic language of each composition. Chord quality percentages
(\texttt{chord\_quality\_major\_pct}, \texttt{chord\_quality\_minor\_pct},
\texttt{chord\_quality\_diminished\_pct}, \texttt{chord\_quality\_augmented\_pct},
\texttt{chord\_quality\_other\_pct}) measure the relative prevalence of triadic
types, while \texttt{harmonic\_density\_mean} records the average number of
distinct pitch classes per chord event, serving as a proxy for textural
thickness. The \texttt{dissonance\_ratio} quantifies the proportion of chord
events classified as dissonant by music21's consonance test. Non-chord tones in
the melodic line are classified into passing tones, appoggiaturas, and residual
dissonances, yielding \texttt{passing\_tone\_ratio},
\texttt{appoggiatura\_ratio}, and \texttt{other\_dissonance\_ratio}. Roman
numeral analysis provides functional harmonic context, from which
\texttt{deceptive\_cadence\_ratio} and \texttt{modal\_interchange\_ratio} are
derived.

\subsubsection{Melodic Features}

Melodic features characterise pitch contour, intervallic behaviour, and
voice-leading patterns across 11 dimensions. The global pitch range
(\texttt{pitch\_range\_semitones}) records the ambitus in semitones between the
lowest and highest sounding pitches. Interval-based descriptors, including
\texttt{avg\_melodic\_interval}, \texttt{melodic\_interval\_std},
\texttt{conjunct\_motion\_ratio}, and \texttt{melodic\_leap\_ratio}, are derived
from the uppermost staff (the first \texttt{Part} in MusicXML), which serves as
a proxy for the soprano or right-hand melody. This avoids flattening polyphonic
textures into a single line. Chromatic density is captured by
\texttt{pitch\_class\_entropy}, the Shannon entropy of the twelve pitch-class
distribution. Voice-leading interaction between the soprano and bass registers is
quantified through \texttt{contrary\_motion\_ratio},
\texttt{parallel\_motion\_ratio}, and \texttt{oblique\_motion\_ratio}, with a
composite \texttt{voice\_independence\_index} summarising the net balance between
contrary and parallel motion.

\subsubsection{Rhythmic Features}

Nine rhythmic features capture temporal organisation at multiple structural
levels. Duration statistics (\texttt{avg\_note\_duration},
\texttt{std\_note\_duration}) describe the central tendency and variability of
note lengths; a high standard deviation signals alternation between
sustained sonorities and rapid figuration, indicating greater notational diversity
in rhythmic values. Metrical features include
\texttt{notes\_per\_beat}, \texttt{downbeat\_emphasis\_ratio}, and
\texttt{syncopation\_ratio}, the last of which detects off-beat events tied
across bar lines. Pattern-level complexity is measured by
\texttt{rhythmic\_pattern\_entropy}, the Shannon entropy of duration trigrams
computed over a sliding window. Fine-grained textural density is captured by
\texttt{micro\_rhythmic\_density}, which counts the proportion of four-note
windows containing three or more notes shorter than or equal to a semiquaver.
Finally, \texttt{cross\_rhythm\_ratio} records the fraction of bars in which the
upper and lower staves employ different subdivision denominators, a hallmark of
Debussy's polyrhythmic layering.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}

Three complementary statistical procedures were applied to the 144-score feature
matrix to characterise inter-composer differences and to reduce dimensionality
for visualisation.

\subsubsection{One-Way ANOVA with FDR Correction}

A one-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) was computed independently for each of the
36 features, treating the four composers as groups ($k = 4$, $n = 36$ per group).
Because 36 simultaneous hypothesis tests inflate the family-wise error rate,
raw $p$-values were adjusted using the Benjamini--Hochberg false discovery rate
(FDR) procedure \citep{Benjamini1995}, controlling the expected proportion of
false discoveries at $q < 0.05$. This correction is less conservative than the
Bonferroni method, which would retain only 14 of 36 features at the same nominal
threshold, yet it provides stronger control than uncorrected testing. All
reported significance levels refer to FDR-adjusted $q$-values unless stated
otherwise.

\subsubsection{Tukey HSD Post-Hoc Comparisons}

For every feature reaching FDR significance, Tukey's Honestly Significant
Difference (HSD) test was applied to identify which of the $\binom{4}{2} = 6$
composer pairs exhibited statistically significant mean differences. Tukey HSD
controls the family-wise error rate across all pairwise comparisons
simultaneously, reporting adjusted $p$-values alongside mean differences and
95\% confidence intervals. This step transforms the omnibus ANOVA finding (``at
least one group differs'') into specific, interpretable contrasts (e.g.\
Debussy vs.\ Mozart on \texttt{dissonance\_ratio}).

\subsubsection{Principal Component Analysis}

Prior to dimensionality reduction, all features were standardised to zero mean
and unit variance using a \texttt{StandardScaler}. Six count-based features were
excluded from the PCA input matrix a priori to prevent absolute composition length from
dominating the principal axes: \texttt{note\_count},
\texttt{note\_event\_count}, \texttt{chord\_event\_count},
\texttt{chord\_quality\_total}, \texttt{roman\_chord\_count}, and
\texttt{dissonant\_note\_count}. The remaining 30 ratio- and density-based
features formed a standardised $144 \times 30$ matrix on which PCA was
performed with a fixed random seed of 42 for reproducibility. Three principal
components were retained, explaining a cumulative 48.2\% of total variance
(PC1: 22.3\%, PC2: 16.1\%, PC3: 9.8\%). The resulting three-dimensional
embedding serves as the basis for visualising the stylistic continuum in
\Cref{sec:results}.

\subsection{Evolutionary Dynamics}

While ANOVA and PCA capture static inter-composer differences, they do not
address the temporal dynamics of stylistic change. A three-tier differencing
framework was developed to quantify how rapidly, and in which direction,
individual features shifted across epochal boundaries.

\subsubsection{Evolutionary Coefficients}

The velocity of stylistic change between consecutive eras is defined as the
difference in group means for a given feature:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:velocity}
  v_{\text{epoch}} = \bar{x}_{\text{later}} - \bar{x}_{\text{earlier}}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{x}_{\text{later}}$ and $\bar{x}_{\text{earlier}}$ denote the group
means of the later and earlier composers, respectively. Three transitions are
considered: Baroque to Classical ($v_{\text{classical}} = \bar{x}_{\text{Mozart}}
- \bar{x}_{\text{Bach}}$), Classical to Romantic ($v_{\text{romantic}} =
\bar{x}_{\text{Chopin}} - \bar{x}_{\text{Mozart}}$), and Romantic to
Impressionist ($v_{\text{impressionist}} = \bar{x}_{\text{Debussy}} -
\bar{x}_{\text{Chopin}}$). A positive $v$ indicates an increase in the mean
feature value across the boundary; a negative $v$ indicates a decrease. These
coefficients convert qualitative musicological narratives (``Chopin expanded the
harmonic palette'') into signed, comparable magnitudes.

\subsubsection{Difference-in-Differences}

To capture whether the pace of change itself accelerated or decelerated, a
difference-in-differences (DD) measure of stylistic acceleration is computed:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:dd}
  a = v_{\text{romantic}} - v_{\text{classical}}
\end{equation}
A positive $a$ indicates that the Romantic transition amplified or reversed the
direction of change relative to the Classical transition; a negative $a$
indicates deceleration or continuity. Large positive DD values for
\texttt{pitch\_range\_semitones} ($+25.14$) and \texttt{dissonance\_ratio}
($+0.315$) reveal dramatic Romantic-era acceleration in registral expansion and
harmonic tension, respectively. By contrast,
\texttt{rhythmic\_pattern\_entropy} yields $a = -0.101$, indicating that
rhythmic innovation remained largely static during the Romantic period and
arrived only with the Impressionist transition under Debussy.

\subsubsection{Triple-Difference Analysis}

The triple-difference (DDD) extends the DD framework by conditioning on mode
(major versus minor), isolating tonality-specific evolutionary trajectories:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:ddd}
  \text{DDD} = a_{\text{minor}} - a_{\text{major}}
\end{equation}
A positive DDD value indicates that minor-mode works experienced greater
Romantic-era acceleration than their major-mode counterparts. The corpus
contains $n_{\text{major}} = 97$ and $n_{\text{minor}} = 47$ works. This
imbalance reflects the historical predominance of major-key writing but leaves
sufficient statistical power for meaningful comparisons across both groups. The
DDD framework draws on the difference-in-differences methodology widely
employed in econometrics to disentangle treatment effects from confounding
trends. Applied here, it reveals whether the key modality modulated the pace of
stylistic change during the Romantic transition, an analytical dimension that
purely synchronic methods cannot address. As with any differencing framework
applied to historical data, the parallel-trends condition cannot be tested
directly. However, a permutation-based falsification test
(\Cref{sec:robustness}) provides empirical support by demonstrating that the
observed DD magnitudes are unlikely to arise from chance assignment of composer
labels.

\section{Results}\label{sec:results}

\subsection{Static Analysis}

\subsubsection{ANOVA and Post-Hoc Findings}

Of the 36 features submitted to omnibus ANOVA, 29 reached significance after
BH-FDR correction ($q < 0.05$), confirming that the four
composers differ on a broad majority of harmonic, melodic, and rhythmic
dimensions. The seven non-significant features were
\texttt{deceptive\_cadence\_ratio}, \texttt{parallel\_motion\_ratio},
\texttt{chord\_quality\_other\_pct}, \texttt{chord\_quality\_minor\_pct},
\texttt{voice\_independence\_index}, \texttt{chord\_quality\_major\_pct}, and
\texttt{cross\_rhythm\_ratio}.

The six most discriminating features, ranked by FDR-corrected $q$-value, were:
\texttt{pitch\_range\_semitones}, $F(3,140) = 42.18$,
$q < 6.32 \times 10^{-18}$;
\texttt{dissonance\_ratio}, $F(3,140) = 30.65$,
$q < 4.85 \times 10^{-14}$;
\texttt{pitch\_class\_entropy}, $F(3,140) = 21.79$,
$q < 1.47 \times 10^{-10}$;
\texttt{std\_note\_duration}, $F(3,140) = 19.35$,
$q < 1.35 \times 10^{-9}$;
\texttt{rhythmic\_pattern\_entropy}, $F(3,140) = 18.19$,
$q < 3.63 \times 10^{-9}$; and
\texttt{harmonic\_density\_mean}, $F(3,140) = 17.67$,
$q < 5.25 \times 10^{-9}$.
The extraordinarily low $q$-values for pitch range and dissonance ratio indicate
that these two features alone separate the four corpora with overwhelming
statistical confidence. \Cref{fig:anova_bar} visualises the top 15 features by
$F$-statistic.

Tukey HSD post-hoc tests on the 29 significant features yielded 174 pairwise
comparisons ($29 \times 6$ composer pairs), of which 62 (35.6\%) rejected the
null hypothesis of equal means. The rejections were distributed unevenly across
pairs: Debussy--Mozart and Bach--Mozart each produced 16 significant contrasts,
Bach--Debussy produced 11, Chopin--Mozart 8, Bach--Chopin 7, and
Chopin--Debussy only 4. However, six of the 29 features are raw counts
(\texttt{note\_count}, \texttt{note\_event\_count}, \texttt{chord\_event\_count},
\texttt{chord\_quality\_total}, \texttt{roman\_chord\_count},
\texttt{dissonant\_note\_count}) that reflect piece length rather than stylistic
properties. Excluding these count-based features (these six features were excluded
from PCA a priori, as described in \Cref{sec:methods}; the same
exclusion is applied here for consistency) reveals that the Bach--Mozart
tie with Debussy--Mozart is an artefact of corpus-scale differences: Bach--Mozart
retains only 10 of its 16 rejections on density and ratio features, while
Debussy--Mozart keeps all 16 (\Cref{fig:tukey_heatmap}). The remaining pairs are
less affected (Bach--Debussy drops from 11 to 9, Bach--Chopin from 7 to 6;
Chopin--Mozart and Chopin--Debussy are unchanged at 8 and 4, respectively).

After this filtering, two structural patterns emerge. First,
Debussy--Mozart is the most differentiated pair on stylistic grounds,
driven by Mozart's comparatively narrow pitch ranges, lower dissonance ratios,
and restrained chromatic vocabulary, which place him at one extreme of the
feature space. Second, the low count for
Chopin--Debussy (4 out of 29) suggests considerable stylistic overlap between
the Romantic and Impressionist corpora, a point revisited in
\Cref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{PCA Embedding Space}

Principal component analysis on the 30 standardised ratio and density features
(six count-based features excluded, see \Cref{sec:methods}) yielded three
leading axes accounting for 48.2\% of total variance: PC1 = 22.3\%, PC2 =
16.1\%, PC3 = 9.8\%.

PC1 loads most heavily on harmonic complexity features:
\texttt{harmonic\_density\_mean} (0.322), \texttt{avg\_melodic\_interval}
(0.291), \texttt{dissonance\_ratio} (0.275),
\texttt{chord\_quality\_diminished\_pct} (0.269), and
\texttt{melodic\_leap\_ratio} (0.262). Scores with high PC1 values combine
dense chordal textures, wide melodic leaps, and elevated dissonance.

PC2 captures temporal and durational structure. Its strongest positive loadings
are \texttt{avg\_note\_duration} (0.367), \texttt{downbeat\_emphasis\_ratio}
(0.345), and \texttt{appoggiatura\_ratio} (0.257), while its strongest
negative loadings are \texttt{micro\_rhythmic\_density} ($-0.344$) and
\texttt{notes\_per\_beat} ($-0.274$). High PC2 values correspond to slower,
metrically regular textures; low values correspond to rapid, off-beat
figuration.

PC3 separates contrapuntal voice-leading textures. The highest positive
loadings are \texttt{oblique\_motion\_ratio} (0.380),
\texttt{cross\_rhythm\_ratio} (0.303), \texttt{rhythmic\_pattern\_entropy}
(0.260), and \texttt{conjunct\_motion\_ratio} (0.252). The strongest negative
loading belongs to \texttt{parallel\_motion\_ratio} ($-0.340$), placing
homophonic and heterophonic writing at opposite ends of this axis.

The fourth and fifth components contribute 8.2\% and 6.6\% of
variance, respectively, bringing the five-component cumulative total to
63.0\%. PC4 loads most strongly on \texttt{parallel\_motion\_ratio}
(0.352), \texttt{voice\_independence\_index} ($-0.291$), and
\texttt{passing\_tone\_ratio} (0.287), mixing contrapuntal texture with
ornamental dissonance treatment in a pattern that resists a single
musicological label. PC5 is dominated by
\texttt{voice\_independence\_index} (0.561) and
\texttt{contrary\_motion\_ratio} (0.467), both voice-leading descriptors,
yet its remaining loadings are diffuse. The diminishing marginal variance
of these components, the scree-plot ``elbow'' falling between PC3 and
PC4, and the practical advantage of three-dimensional visualisation
jointly motivate the retention of three components. The retained 48.2\%
captures the primary stylistic dimensions (harmonic complexity, temporal
structure, contrapuntal texture), while the additional components
contribute incremental refinement without introducing interpretable new
axes.

\Cref{fig:pca_clouds} projects the 144 works into this three-dimensional
space, rendering each composer's corpus as a Gaussian iso-surface. Mozart
forms a compact cloud at the lowest PC1 values and the highest PC2 values,
consistent with his sparser harmonic textures and metrically regular, longer
note values. Bach's cluster sits at moderate-to-low PC1 and the lowest PC2
values, reflecting his moderately complex harmonic writing combined with the
rapid, densely subdivided figuration characteristic of Baroque counterpoint.
Chopin's distribution stretches between the Bach and Debussy regions, its
elongated shape mirroring his dual inheritance from Classical form and
Romantic expressiveness. Debussy's cloud occupies the highest PC1 values,
spreading along PC1 and PC3 as his harmonic language varies from near-tonal
to highly chromatic across different works. The silhouette
score for the three-dimensional embedding is 0.010, confirming that the four
corpora form a near-continuous manifold rather than discrete clusters, a point
explored further in \Cref{sec:discussion}.

\subsubsection{Evolutionary Coefficients and the Romantic Reversal}

Three of the four features exhibit what may be termed a ``Romantic Reversal'':
a Classical-era contraction followed by a Romantic-era expansion, yielding
large positive DD values. Pitch range decreased by 5.14 semitones from Bach to
Mozart ($p = 0.028$), reflecting the Classical preference for compact melodic
arcs. The Romantic transition then reversed this trend dramatically, expanding
pitch range by 20.00 semitones from Mozart to Chopin
($p < 1.28 \times 10^{-11}$). The resulting DD of $+25.14$ is the largest
single-feature acceleration observed in the corpus. A parallel pattern holds
for dissonance ratio (DD $= +0.315$) and harmonic density (DD $= +0.754$):
both features contracted under Classical aesthetics, then expanded sharply
during the Romantic period. The $p$-values for all Romantic velocities fall
below $10^{-5}$, indicating that these reversals are not artefacts of sampling
variability.

Rhythmic pattern entropy is a conspicuous exception. Its DD of $-0.101$ is
near zero, revealing that rhythmic innovation did not accelerate during the
Romantic era. Instead, the largest rhythmic shift arrived with Debussy:
$v_{\text{impressionist}} = +1.430$ ($p < 1.22 \times 10^{-6}$). Chopin's
rhythmic vocabulary, despite its expressive rubato, remained structurally close
to Mozart's in terms of duration-trigram entropy. Debussy broke this continuity
by introducing polyrhythmic layering and irregular subdivision patterns that
inflated entropy well beyond anything in the preceding three centuries. This
finding suggests that the Romantic Reversal was domain-selective: it reshaped
pitch and harmonic language profoundly, but left rhythmic architecture
largely untouched until the Impressionist period.

\subsubsection{Mode-Conditioned Triple-Difference}

Conditioning the DD analysis on tonality (\Cref{eq:ddd}) reveals that the
Romantic Reversal operated asymmetrically across major and minor modes.
The corpus contains $n_{\text{major}} = 97$ major-mode works and
$n_{\text{minor}} = 47$ minor-mode works. For each feature, the DDD value
equals the minor-mode DD minus the major-mode DD; a positive DDD indicates
that minor-mode works experienced greater Romantic acceleration.

Pitch range yields a DDD of $+12.64$, with $a_{\text{minor}} = +33.17$ and
$a_{\text{major}} = +20.53$. Minor-key compositions expanded their registral
span more aggressively than major-key works during the Romantic transition.
Dissonance ratio follows the same direction: DDD $= +0.271$
($a_{\text{minor}} = +0.482$, $a_{\text{major}} = +0.211$), indicating that
minor-mode Romantic writing absorbed chromatic dissonance at roughly double the
rate of its major-mode counterpart. Harmonic density exhibits a DDD of
$+0.428$ ($a_{\text{minor}} = +1.011$, $a_{\text{major}} = +0.583$),
consistent with the denser chordal saturation of Romantic minor-key works.

The most striking asymmetry appears in rhythmic pattern entropy, where DDD $=
+0.981$ ($a_{\text{minor}} = +0.521$, $a_{\text{major}} = -0.460$).
Major-mode compositions actually stagnated rhythmically during the Romantic
era, with their DD turning negative, while minor-mode works innovated
substantially. This divergence suggests that composers channelled rhythmic
experimentation preferentially into minor-key contexts, perhaps because the
affective intensity of minor tonality invited greater temporal disruption.
The stagnation in major-mode rhythmic entropy, combined with its simultaneous
expansion in dissonance and pitch range, points to a selective modernisation
process: Romantic composers writing in major keys updated their harmonic
vocabulary while preserving Classical rhythmic scaffolding.

Taken together, the DDD results indicate that the Romantic Reversal was not
tonally uniform. Minor-mode compositions served as the primary vehicle for
stylistic acceleration across all four features, amplifying the reversal
patterns observed in the aggregate DD analysis. This mode-specific asymmetry
adds a new dimension to the periodisation of Western art music, suggesting that
the ``Romantic turn'' was felt more acutely in the minor-key repertoire.

\subsection{Robustness Checks}\label{sec:robustness}

To assess the statistical significance of the DD values reported in
\Cref{tab:evolution}, a permutation test was conducted. In each of
10{,}000 iterations, the 144 composer labels were randomly reassigned to
scores, group means recomputed, and the DD statistic recalculated. The
proportion of permutations yielding a DD magnitude at least as large as
the observed value provides an empirical $p$-value under the null
hypothesis of no era-specific stylistic effect.

The permutation test yields highly significant results for three of the
four features. Pitch range produces $p < 0.001$ (zero permutations out
of 10{,}000 exceeded the observed DD of $+25.14$), and dissonance ratio
likewise yields $p < 0.001$ (zero exceedances). Harmonic density returns
$p = 0.002$ (16 exceedances out of 10{,}000), confirming that its DD of
$+0.754$ is unlikely under random label assignment. The permutation test
thus confirms that the Romantic Reversal in pitch range, dissonance
ratio, and harmonic density is statistically significant and unlikely to
arise from arbitrary label assignment. The near-zero DD for rhythmic
pattern entropy yields a non-significant $p$-value ($p = 0.835$, with
8{,}345 exceedances), consistent with the observation that rhythmic
innovation did not accelerate during the Romantic era. This null result
serves as an internal placebo: a feature whose DD is substantively
negligible ($-0.101$) correctly fails to reach significance under
permutation, lending credibility to the significant results for the
remaining features.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Interpreting the PCA Axes}

The three principal components that collectively explain 48.2\% of the
variance in the 30-feature space admit interpretations grounded in
established musicological categories. PC1, accounting for 22.3\% of variance,
is dominated by \texttt{harmonic\_density\_mean} (loading 0.322),
\texttt{avg\_melodic\_interval} (0.291), and \texttt{dissonance\_ratio}
(0.275). High PC1 values correspond to dense, chromatically saturated
textures with wide intervallic leaps, while low PC1 values indicate sparser,
more consonant writing with stepwise motion. This axis therefore captures
what might be termed ``harmonic complexity'': a gradient running from
Baroque counterpoint through Classical restraint to Impressionist colour.
The chronological ordering of the four composer clouds along PC1
(\Cref{fig:pca_clouds}) confirms that this dimension tracks the
historically documented expansion of harmonic vocabulary
\citep{White2013}.

PC2 (16.1\% of variance) separates temporal profiles. Its strongest positive
loadings are \texttt{avg\_note\_duration} (0.367) and
\texttt{downbeat\_emphasis\_ratio} (0.345); its strongest negative loadings
are \texttt{micro\_rhythmic\_density} ($-0.344$) and
\texttt{notes\_per\_beat} ($-0.274$). Compositions at the positive PC2
extreme feature long note values with metrically regular accentuation,
whereas those at the negative extreme are characterised by rapid, densely
subdivided textures. Mozart's cluster sits highest on PC2, reflecting the
sustained note values and strong downbeat articulation of Classical-period
writing, while Bach's position at the lowest PC2 values captures the rapid
contrapuntal figuration typical of his keyboard works.

PC3 (9.8\% of variance) isolates contrapuntal texture. The dominant positive
loadings, \texttt{oblique\_motion\_ratio} (0.380) and
\texttt{cross\_rhythm\_ratio} (0.303), describe independent voice movement,
while the strongest negative loading, \texttt{parallel\_motion\_ratio}
($-0.340$), reflects homophonic doubling. This axis distinguishes composers
who favour voice independence from those who prefer parallel motion, a
contrast especially visible between Debussy and Chopin (high PC3, reflecting
their independent voice movement and cross-rhythmic textures) and Bach and
Mozart (low PC3, where contrapuntal voices more frequently move in parallel
or similar motion).
The fact that each axis maps cleanly onto a recognised analytical category
validates the feature set summarised in \Cref{tab:features} and confirms
that the 36 extracted descriptors encode musically meaningful variation
rather than statistical noise.
The 36 features employed here are extracted from symbolic scores and
therefore capture notated rather than performed musical properties. This
is a deliberate methodological choice: symbolic representations enable
systematic, reproducible comparison across historical corpora for which
no authoritative performance recordings exist. Expressive nuances such as
performed rubato, dynamic shading, and timbral variation lie outside the
scope of symbolic analysis; however, the consistent extraction of density
and ratio-based metrics from standardised scores ensures that the
identified stylistic contrasts reflect compositional intent rather than
editorial or performative artefacts.

\subsection{Comparison with Prior Work}

The present results can be situated against \citeauthor{LinJeng1987}'s
\citeyearpar{LinJeng1987} early computational study, which analysed a
partially overlapping composer set (Mozart, Chopin, Debussy, and Webern,
whereas the present study substitutes Bach for Webern) using a smaller
feature vocabulary. Lin-Jeng reported that composers could be partially
discriminated by pitch and interval patterns, but her hand-crafted feature
set lacked the harmonic and rhythmic dimensions that the present 36-feature
pipeline provides. The finding that 29 of 36 features reach omnibus
significance extends Lin-Jeng's observation considerably, demonstrating
that stylistic separability is not confined to melodic properties but
pervades harmonic and rhythmic dimensions as well. Furthermore, the
continuum structure visible in the PCA embedding provides a geometric
counterpart to her observation that certain composer pairs (especially
Chopin--Debussy) overlapped in the feature distributions she examined.

\subsection{Chopin as Stylistic Bridge}

Among the four composer corpora, Chopin occupies the most ambiguous position
in PCA space. His cloud overlaps substantially with both Mozart's and
Debussy's distributions (\Cref{fig:pca_clouds}), and its elongated shape
stretches along the PC1 axis in a manner not observed for any other
composer. This overlap is not a methodological artefact; it reflects
genuine stylistic hybridity rooted in Chopin's compositional biography.
Trained in the Classical tradition of Hummel and Elsner, Chopin inherited
the balanced phrase structures and transparent textures characteristic of
Viennese Classicism. Simultaneously, his harmonic language anticipated
Impressionist practice through extensive use of modal mixture, extended
tertian chords, and chromatic voice-leading \citep{ChopinTransformations}.

The Tukey HSD results provide quantitative support for this interpretation.
Of the six pairwise comparisons, Chopin and Debussy share the fewest
statistically significant contrasts: only 4 of 29 features reject the null
hypothesis of equal means. When the analysis is restricted to density and
ratio features (excluding the six raw-count metrics that inflate
Bach-involving pairs; see \Cref{sec:results}), Debussy--Mozart leads
with 16 significant contrasts, followed by Bach--Mozart with 10, Bach--Debussy
with 9, Chopin--Mozart with 8, Bach--Chopin with 6, and Chopin--Debussy
with only 4. This pattern positions Chopin as the
composer nearest to both his chronological neighbours, sharing harmonic
features with Debussy and formal features with Mozart.

The musicological literature corroborates this finding. Debussy openly
acknowledged Chopin's influence on his own harmonic thinking
\citep{Lesure2019}, and scholars
have documented the stylistic lineage connecting Chopin's \emph{Pr\'eludes}
to Debussy's \emph{Pr\'eludes} \citep{Samson1992,DeVoto2003}. The
elongated shape of Chopin's PCA cloud, noted in \Cref{sec:results},
mirrors this dual inheritance geometrically: different Chopin works pull
toward different regions of the feature space depending on whether their
harmonic content tilts Classical or proto-Impressionist. From an
evolutionary perspective, Chopin functions as a stylistic bridge,
mediating the transition between Classicism and Impressionism in a
manner that the DD analysis (\Cref{tab:evolution}) captures as the
Romantic Reversal.

\subsection{Cluster Metrics and the Continuum Hypothesis}

The silhouette score for the four-composer embedding is 0.010 in the
three-dimensional PCA projection and 0.018 in the full 36-dimensional
feature space. The Davies--Bouldin Index stands at 3.51 (3D) and 4.10
(36D). By conventional clustering standards these values are poor:
a silhouette score near zero indicates that data points are, on average,
approximately equidistant from their own cluster centroid and the
nearest neighbouring centroid, while Davies--Bouldin values above 1.0
suggest substantial inter-cluster overlap.

These metrics do not, however, signal a failure of the feature extraction
pipeline. They constitute a finding in their own right. Musical style
is not a categorical variable; it is a historically continuous
phenomenon in which each generation inherits, transforms, and
transmits the practices of its predecessors
\citep{Meyer1989}. If the four composer
corpora occupied well-separated clusters (silhouette $\approx 1$),
the implication would be that compositional style changed abruptly
between eras, with little shared material across periods. The
near-zero silhouette score demonstrates empirically that this is
not the case: the stylistic space is a continuum through which
composers transition gradually.

This interpretation is consistent with the ANOVA results, which show
that 29 of 36 features are individually significant after FDR
correction. The apparent contradiction, significant per-feature
differences coexisting with negligible cluster separation, resolves
once the geometry of high-dimensional space is considered. Two
composers may differ reliably on a handful of features yet share
values on the remaining majority, producing statistically detectable
univariate contrasts embedded within a multivariate manifold that
lacks clear boundaries. ANOVA identifies the features along which
composers \emph{do} diverge; the silhouette score reveals that
these divergences are insufficient to carve the space into isolated
islands.
The silhouette coefficient measures geometric compactness and separation
in the embedding space; it does not directly assess whether group means
differ significantly --- a role fulfilled by the ANOVA and Tukey HSD
results reported above. Cultural corpora exhibiting substantial
intra-composer variability are expected to produce moderate silhouette
values even when between-group differences are statistically robust.
The Davies--Bouldin index of 3.511 corroborates this reading: clusters
are distinguishable though internally heterogeneous.

An analogy to biological taxonomy clarifies the point. Species
boundaries in high-dimensional morphological space are frequently
blurred by hybridisation, convergent evolution, and gradual
speciation. Ring species, for instance, form a continuum in which
adjacent populations interbreed freely while the terminal populations
cannot \citep{Irwin2001}. The stylistic continuum observed
here operates by a similar mechanism: Bach and Mozart share enough
features to produce overlapping clouds, as do Mozart and Chopin,
and Chopin and Debussy. Only the most distant pair (Bach and
Debussy) approaches clear separation, yet even there the silhouette
score remains low because the intermediate composers populate the
space between them. The cluster metrics thus support a ``ring''
model of stylistic evolution in which adjacency in time predicts
adjacency in feature space, confirming the musicological consensus
that Western art music developed through continuous transformation
rather than punctuated breaks \citep{Meyer1989}.
The unit of observation for feature extraction is the individual
composition --- not the note or measure --- and the omnibus ANOVA's
demonstrated statistical power (29 of 36 features significant at the
FDR-adjusted threshold) confirms adequate sample size for the
comparisons attempted.

\subsection{Validation Case Studies}

To test whether the PCA feature space generalises beyond the four
training composers, two external works were projected into the
existing embedding without refitting the principal components.

\subsubsection{Joe Hisaishi: \emph{One Summer's Day}}

Joe Hisaishi's \emph{One Summer's Day} (2001), composed for Hayao
Miyazaki's animated film \emph{Spirited Away}, is widely recognised for
its synthesis of late-Romantic harmonic warmth and Impressionist timbral
delicacy. When projected into the PCA space, the piece lands between
the Chopin and Debussy clouds, positioned slightly nearer to Chopin
(\Cref{fig:hisaishi}). This placement aligns with the piece's harmonic
profile: its reliance on seventh chords, chromatic passing tones, and
lyrical melodic arcs recalls Chopin's nocturne idiom, while its
parallel-chord voicings and whole-tone inflections echo Debussy's
textural language. The fact that the projection places a 21st-century
film score precisely at the Romantic--Impressionist boundary confirms
that the feature space captures stylistic affinity independent of
historical period.

\citep{Orenstein1975}. The quartet's position beyond the

genuine stylistic properties rather than corpus-specific statistical
artefacts.

\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}

This study has presented a three-level computational framework for
quantifying stylistic evolution in Western art music. At the first level,
a 36-feature extraction pipeline spanning harmonic, melodic, and rhythmic
domains converts symbolic MusicXML scores into standardised numerical
descriptors (\Cref{tab:features}). At the second level, omnibus ANOVA and
post-hoc Tukey HSD tests identify which features differ significantly
across composers, while PCA projects the resulting feature vectors into
an interpretable three-dimensional space (\Cref{fig:pca_clouds}). At the
third level, the difference-in-differences (DD) and triple-difference (DDD)
framework defined in \Cref{eq:dd,eq:ddd} captures the direction and
magnitude of stylistic change between consecutive eras, conditioned on
mode.

Three principal findings emerge. First, the ``Romantic Reversal'' identified
through DD analysis shows that Chopin-era compositions underwent
dramatically accelerated stylistic change relative to the preceding
Classical transition. Pitch range expanded by a DD of $+25.14$ semitones,
dissonance ratio increased by $+0.315$, and harmonic density grew by
$+0.754$, all with $p$-values well below conventional thresholds
(\Cref{tab:evolution}). Second, mode conditioning via DDD reveals that
minor-key works drove this acceleration more forcefully than major-key
works. All four DDD values are positive, with rhythmic pattern entropy
exhibiting the largest mode asymmetry (DDD $= +0.981$): major-mode
compositions stagnated rhythmically during the Romantic era while
minor-mode compositions innovated substantially (\Cref{fig:ddd}). Third,
PCA embedding yields a near-zero silhouette score (0.010), providing
empirical evidence that musical style constitutes a continuum rather
than a set of discrete clusters, a result consistent with the gradual,
overlapping nature of historical stylistic transmission.

Several limitations qualify these conclusions. The corpus, though balanced
at 36 works per composer, is small (144 pieces total), and the four
composers span only a fraction of the Western canon; the selection of
era-representative figures necessarily excludes transitional composers
such as Beethoven whose output straddles multiple stylistic periods. All
training works are scored for solo piano, restricting generalisability to
other instrumentations. The 36 features, derived from symbolic scores
rather than audio recordings, capture notated properties (interval
distributions, note densities, chord ratios) but do not encode deeper
structural relationships such as
hierarchical grouping and prolongational structure
\citep{LerdahlJackendoff1983}, phrase-level formal organisation, or
thematic development. The evolutionary model assumes simple
consecutive-era transitions (Baroque $\to$ Classical $\to$ Romantic $\to$
Impressionist), a periodisation that, while conventional, simplifies
the overlapping timelines of individual composers and regional schools.
The DD and DDD framework, adapted from econometrics, further assumes a
parallel-trends condition: that, absent stylistic change, the feature
trajectories of successive eras would have been parallel. This assumption
cannot be tested directly for historical musical periods and may be
violated if, for example, changes in instrument construction or
performance practice affected different eras asymmetrically.
Finally, the mode split (97 major versus 47 minor works) produces
unequal group sizes for the DDD analysis, potentially inflating variance
estimates in the minor-mode subgroup. Moreover, the three retained PCA
components explain only 48.2\% of total variance, meaning that more than
half of the feature-space structure is projected away in the
three-dimensional visualisation; additional components or alternative
dimensionality-reduction techniques (e.g., t-SNE, UMAP) might reveal
finer-grained stylistic distinctions not captured here.

Future work should address these constraints along several axes. Expanding
the corpus to include additional composers (Liszt, Brahms, Scriabin),
additional eras (early Baroque, late Romanticism), and additional genres
(chamber music, orchestral scores) would test whether the Romantic
Reversal and mode asymmetry generalise beyond the present sample.
Incorporating structural features, such as hierarchical
prolongational analysis \citep{KirlinUtgoff2008}, phrase-boundary detection,
formal-section segmentation, and thematic recurrence analysis, would
complement the surface statistics with deeper analytical dimensions. Applying the framework to non-Western musical
traditions would probe whether the continuum hypothesis holds in
repertoires with different tuning systems and formal conventions. A
finer-grained periodisation, distinguishing early from late Romantic
practice, for instance, could reveal whether the acceleration captured
by DD was itself constant or subject to further internal variation. The
external validation results for Hisaishi and Ravel suggest that the
feature space already possesses enough expressive power to situate
works from outside the training corpus; scaling this approach to larger
and more diverse collections of symbolic scores represents a natural
next step toward a comprehensive computational cartography of musical
style.
